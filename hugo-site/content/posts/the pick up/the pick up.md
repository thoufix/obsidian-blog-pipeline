It started at 6 a.m., but the tension had already begun long before that.

I woke up with one clear thought repeating in my mind — _this time should not go like last time_. The previous trip had already left its mark on me. The same confusion, the same misalignment, the same ending. I didn’t want to relive that.

But the more I thought about avoiding it, the more it felt like something waiting to happen again.

Yesterday, I had sent her the map. She pointed to a location where she might get down. Not exactly certain, but close enough. Still, there was uncertainty — she herself wasn’t sure where the bus would finally stop.

That uncertainty stayed in my head.

I built a plan around it — _wherever she gets down, she can wait… I’ll find her_. It sounded simple in my mind. Controlled. Manageable.

But reality didn’t follow that plan.

When the moment came, I reached the location she had marked — and I didn’t stop there.

Something in me said buses wouldn’t stop at that exact point. I moved ahead, looking for something that felt more “correct.” I stopped near a place that looked like a proper omni bus stop and even confirmed it with a shop owner.

It felt logical.

It felt right.

It was wrong.

By the time she got down, everything shifted.

She was calm inside the bus. But the moment she stepped out, something changed. Her tone. The urgency. The pressure. It all hit at once.

Messages came in fast. Words carried weight. There was no time to think.

Then came the line:  
“Opposite Holiday Inn.”

Opposite.

A simple word, but in that moment, it split into multiple possibilities. Across the road? In front of it? Near it? Behind it?

I couldn’t ask. There was no space for questions. Only movement.

My thoughts started racing.

_This is happening again._

The exact situation I wanted to avoid was unfolding in front of me — almost identically. The same confusion. The same misalignment. The same rising tension.

It felt less like a mistake and more like a pattern I was being pulled back into.

I panicked.

Every decision after that felt rushed, uncertain, and heavy. I wasn’t thinking clearly anymore. I was reacting.

I hadn’t slept the whole night. My mind was already worn out, and now it was trying to keep up with something it couldn’t control.

And then I lost it.

I raised my voice.

Not because I wanted to hurt, but because I felt trapped — between what I thought should happen and what was actually happening.

Even after everything, while driving back, I took the wrong route again. Another mistake. Another correction from her.

It only reinforced what I was already feeling — that I was not in control of the situation, or even the direction.

But the moment that stayed with me wasn’t the argument.

It was Shayan.

He was there through all of it.

He asked, “Mommy, what happened?”

He noticed.

He saw a version of me he hadn’t seen before.

And I didn’t even look at him. I didn’t greet him. I didn’t connect with him.

In that entire storm, I forgot to be present as a father.

That is what lingers the most.

Looking back, it doesn’t feel like a single incident.

It feels like a loop — something I knew, something I tried to avoid, and something that still happened exactly the way I feared it would.

Not because it was inevitable, but because somewhere in the confusion, the pressure, and the fear of repeating it… I walked right back into it.
